# Q4 Experiment Log – NotebookLM Evaluation

## Tool Evaluated

NotebookLM was evaluated as a cloud-based AI research and note-taking assistant.

## Purpose

The purpose of the experiment was to test whether NotebookLM could support academic and professional ESG workflows by summarising uploaded sources, answering source-based questions, generating study materials, creating a briefing note, and identifying key themes.

## Scenario

A university operations or sustainability team is preparing an ESG and Responsible AI briefing. NotebookLM is used to analyse uploaded sources related to ESG disclosure reliability, greenwashing prevention, and responsible AI governance.

## Sources Uploaded

- ScienceDirect article on greenwashing prevention in ESG disclosures. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0275531924005130 
- CSIRO Responsible AI ESG Framework for investors. https://www.csiro.au/en/research/technology-space/ai/responsible-ai/rai-esg-framework-for-investors

## Prompts Tested

### Prompt 1: Source Summary

Summarise the uploaded sources in 5 concise bullet points for a university student preparing an ESG and responsible AI report.

### Prompt 2: Source-Based Q&A

According to the uploaded sources, why are ESG disclosures vulnerable to greenwashing, and how can responsible AI governance support more reliable ESG decision-making?

### Prompt 3: Study Guide

Create a short study guide explaining greenwashing prevention, ESG disclosure reliability, responsible AI risks, and the role of governance in AI-enabled ESG analytics.

### Prompt 4: Briefing Note

Create a one-page briefing note for a university operations manager explaining how AI tools could support ESG reporting while managing greenwashing, privacy, bias, hallucination, and governance risks.

### Prompt 5: Key Themes

Identify the main themes across the uploaded sources and explain how they relate to ESG operations, cloud analytics, responsible AI, and sustainability reporting.

## Observations

NotebookLM produced clear and well-structured outputs. The source summary and Q&A responses were useful for understanding the relationship between ESG disclosure reliability, greenwashing risk, and responsible AI governance. The study guide was helpful for organising academic concepts, while the briefing note demonstrated how the tool could support professional communication.

A strength of NotebookLM was that its responses included source references, making it easier to check whether claims were supported by the uploaded documents. Another useful observation was that NotebookLM noted when cloud analytics was not directly discussed in the uploaded sources, which showed awareness of evidence boundaries.

## Limitations

NotebookLM still requires human verification. Its outputs can sound polished, but users need to check source support and avoid relying on summaries alone. There is also a risk that prompts may ask about concepts beyond the uploaded sources, which could lead to over-generalised or unsupported claims. For academic use, NotebookLM should support reading and analysis, not replace critical thinking.

## Conclusion

NotebookLM is suitable for limited academic and professional support use. It can help organise sources, summarise key ideas, generate study materials, and prepare briefing notes. However, it should be used with source checking, human judgement, and responsible data handling practices.
